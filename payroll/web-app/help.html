Easy Paychecks Help

Got questions about our Paycheck Calculator? We’ve got answers!

State

Why do I have to pick a state?

It’s super important to choose your state first so that the calculator can include the state taxes (and local taxes, if any) that must be withheld from an employee’s paycheck. We can calculate paychecks only for employees who live and work in the same state.

What taxes am I responsible for?

As an employer, you’re responsible for the following:

Employee Taxes — We calculate employee taxes for you on paychecks. Employee taxes can include federal, Social Security, Medicare and, in many states, state income or disability taxes. Additionally, some employees owe local taxes depending on their location of residence or worksite location. You report and pay these to the government agencies on your employee’s behalf.

Employer Taxes — As an employer, you’re also responsible for some additional taxes that we do not calculate, including the employer’s portion of Social Security and Medicare, federal unemployment (FUTA) and, in many states, state unemployment (SUI). You report and pay these to the government agencies.

Pay Information

Pay Date

Can I enter dates in the future or dates from last year?

Yes and yes! As long as the future date is within the same calendar year – we don’t know the 2014 tax rates yet. (You can calculate checks farther in the future, but we can’t be sure the taxes will be accurate.) Past dates should be accurate for the last 5 years.

Pay Period

Why do I have to pick a pay period?

It’s important to use a consistent and reliable pay schedule so that tax calculations are correct. Taxes calculated for a weekly check, for example, will come out slightly different than a biweekly check. It also helps you keep payroll taxes and accounting straight throughout the year.

Why don’t you offer more pay periods like monthly or quarterly?

We’d love to, but our crew of developers is pretty small! Unfortunately, if you pay someone quarterly, annually or by project, your tax calculations won’t be correct using our calculator.

Pay Type

What about overtime, bonuses, and commissions?

You can find them in the next section under Additional Pay. (Please keep in mind, if you pay someone annually or by project, your tax calculations won’t be correct using our calculator.)

What about deductions like garnishments or health insurance?

Sorry, but we don’t cover deductions! If you really need deductions and contributions, you should try one of our Intuit Payroll options.

Hourly – Hours Worked

How do I enter overtime hours?

In the Hours Worked box, enter the regular hours. Then click Additional Pay, and enter the remaining overtime hours. (For more info, see Additional Pay section.)

Hourly – Pay Rate

Can I enter other pay rates?

We support hourly pay rates only. Sorry, no per diem (per day), per unit, or project rates.

Salaried – Pay Amount

Should I enter only the gross salary amount for this pay period?

Yes! That’s perfect. We’ll calculate all the taxes and show you the net check amount.

Gross YTD (Year-to-Date)

What if I don’t have the gross year-to-date amount?

If you don’t enter the year-to-date amount, our calculator might calculate too much tax. Some state and federal taxes have an annual maximum amount for earned income, also called a ‘wage base’. Once an employee reaches the annual maximum, they don’t need to pay any more of that tax for the remainder of the year. For example, the Social Security tax wage base is $113,700 for 2013. If your employee has already earned more than that amount year-to-date, then they don’t need to pay any more Social Security until the next tax year begins. Without a year-to-date amount, our calculator won’t know if a maximum has been reached.

Additional Pay

How do I know which hours are overtime?

In general, if you chose weekly for the pay period, enter up to 40 hours in the Hours Worked field, then enter the remaining overtime hours in Additional Pay > Overtime worked. If you chose biweekly for the pay period, you would pay overtime based on a 40 hour work week for each separate week. Any hours worked over 40 in a single week would be overtime hours.

Each state has its own laws and guidelines that determine normal and overtime hours. For details about the overtime laws in your state, check the state labor agency list.

How is overtime calculated?

Our calculator uses an overtime rate of 1.5 times the employee’s regular pay rate for every overtime hour worked. (Double-time is not supported.)

How are commission and bonuses calculated?

Bonuses and commissions are added to, and calculated with, the employee’s regular pay. For example, let’s say an employee works 40 hours during a pay period and also gets a $100.00 sales commission. When you create the employee’s paycheck, you enter 40 hours worked and enter $100.00 in the commission field. The calculator adds the $100.00 commission amount to the employee’s regular pay when calculating the employee’s paycheck.

What about reimbursing employees for expenses?

Since most reimbursements aren’t considered wages, they aren’t generally taxed or included on employee paychecks.

Federal Withholdings

How do I know what to enter for these questions?

Have your employees fill out the federal W-4 form (PDF) and your state’s withholding form. (If there is one; many states don’t have separate forms. Check the State Withholding section for more info.). The filing status and the allowances or exemptions entered on the form(s) help us figure out federal and state taxes. Remember to keep the completed forms in your company records.

What if my employee hasn’t filled out the form yet?

You can still calculate paychecks. Until you have a completed form from your employee, we’ll use the highest agency default rates when calculating your employee’s paycheck.

What if my employee says they don’t have to pay taxes?

If your employee writes 'Exempt' in box 7, then no federal income taxes are withheld. (Exempt employees, with just a few exceptions, will still continue to pay Social Security and Medicare taxes.) Different states use different terminology for employees who do not need to pay taxes. Some states use 'zero percent' or 'exempt'. If your employee claims zero percent or exempt status, choose Do Not Withhold for their federal and state filing status. If you’re not sure or don’t have the forms, choose a filing status like Single instead.

State (and Local) Withholdings

Why do you ask location questions like where my employee lives or works?

For many states, counties, and localities, this information is required for accurate paychecks. (If it wasn’t required, we promise we wouldn’t ask!) Knowing where your employee lives or works helps us to calculate local taxes.

What if my employee hasn’t filled out the state withholding form yet?

You can still calculate paychecks. Until you have a completed form from your employee (only for state’s where they’re required), we’ll use the highest agency default rates when calculating your employee’s paycheck.

New York

How can I find the NY state withholding form?

Get the New York state withholding Form IT-2104. (PDF)

What about NY local taxes?

Currently, employees who live in New York City or the City of Yonkers are subject to a local resident tax. Employees who work in Yonkers are also taxed on the money they earn in the City of Yonkers. If you’re not sure whether the employee is considered a resident of New York City or Yonkers, check the employee’s completed Form IT-2104 (NY state withholding form) or contact the state agency.

What about New York state disability (SDI)?

Sorry, but we don’t calculate New York state disability insurance (SDI). New York SDI can either be paid entirely by the employer or jointly by the employer and employee. To offset the cost of providing the state disability benefit to your employee, New York allows you to deduct up to 60 cents per week (maximum) from the employee’s paychecks. For details, see Disability Benefits on the New York State Insurance Fund (NYSIF) website.
